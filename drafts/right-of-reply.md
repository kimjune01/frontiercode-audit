# Right of reply: an external audit of FrontierCode

*Draft. Not sent. Target: FrontierCode team at Cognition (research contact), with a copy as an issue on kimjune01/frontiercode-audit for the public record. For an email paste, strip the bold markers.*

Hello. I audit coding benchmarks (SWE-bench Verified, DeepSWE, SWE-bench Pro, Terminal-Bench, MirrorCode; method at https://june.kim/how-to-audit-a-benchmark). I've completed a desk audit of FrontierCode from your published pages and one measurement of my own, and I'm sending you the findings before I publish anything. Corrections welcome; I'll link your response from the audit.

Full audit with receipts: https://github.com/kimjune01/frontiercode-audit

**What holds up.** Your diagnosis of SWE-bench's false-positive problem matches what my own audits keep finding. Reverse-classical testing (agent-written tests must fail on the base commit) is a good deterministic invention. The hack-report step, four-solution rubric calibration, and researchers solving tasks themselves are QC most benchmarks lack. The private task set is an anti-contamination credit.

**The main finding.** The headline asks "would the maintainer actually merge this PR?" but the grader sees the patch in its task context and none of the PR-level information maintainers use: discussion, description, contributor identity, policy compliance, duplication, timing. I measured what that omission costs on a population where the outcomes are known: 98 closed-unmerged PRs from an AI-assisted contribution pipeline. Coders re-read every closure thread, and a second coder from a different model family blind-coded the extracted thread evidence for the 59 confidently-classified maintainer/bot closures. In at least 52 of 59, both coders found an independently sufficient stated cause outside your six rubric axes and no sufficient diff-axis cause: AI identity (one closer wrote "Closing without a review"), policy and template compliance, supersession, duplication, interaction cadence, description reasoning ("the summary doesn't explain why certain decisions were made"). Both coders found a diff-quality cause decisive in exactly 3. Four "closed" PRs were accepted work, cherry-picked or ported with credit. The bound: this population is one AI-assisted contributor shipping mostly small fixes, which enriches identity closures, and it is far from Diamond-scale tasks, where code defects may dominate. What survives the bound: off-diff causes can dominate a real contribution population, and I found no published criterion-validity number connecting FrontierCode verdicts to actual maintainer decisions, which is the number "trust it to evaluate the production readiness of their strongest models" presumes. Protocol and coded data are in the repo.

**Findings you can check in an afternoon.**

1. One underdetermined requirement accounts for both failed blockers in the only public task-level grading receipt (the leaderboard demo). The brief's operative sentence is "Use this new function in every instance of warning: <message> messages". On a literal reading, only lines carrying the warning: prefix are instances, and the failed solution converted every one of those; the graded requirement is the broader whole-message reading, which no agent-visible text pins. Your commentary notes the two solutions are "behaviorally the same."
2. The grading oracle's model is never named. Three of your grading methods put an LLM inside the verdict (mutagent adaptation, semantic scope, code-quality prompt), and every published reference says only "an LLM": no name, no version, no published pinning policy. Readers cannot determine whether judge drift affects cross-revision comparisons, and cannot evaluate the judge's independence from the systems it grades.
3. The published materials do not establish that adjacent leaderboard gaps exceed grader noise. On the board as captured 2026-07-17, ranks 4 through 7 (GPT-5.5, Sonnet 5, Grok 4.5, SWE-1.7) sit within 0.7 points. mean@5 samples the agent's variance and leaves the grader's unmeasured, and a grader flip on a blocker criterion moves a trial between zero and its otherwise earned score.
4. Each leaderboard entry is a jointly varying model-harness-effort configuration (your chart legend: "marker shapes distinguish harnesses"; Epoch's export keeps each model's harness and best-performing reasoning effort), and the numbered column reads as a model ranking. The confound's existence is visible from the published pages; its size is the open question an ablation would settle.
5. Deprecated Diamond scores still circulate as the benchmark's public numbers; Epoch's chart reports the Diamond score today. No disclosed reason for the deprecation, no guidance on whether historical Diamond scores compare to current Main.
6. I found no ownership or independence statement on the benchmark, leaderboard, methodology, or SWE-1.7 pages as reviewed 2026-07-17, while SWE-1.7 is scored on the board and its launch post says the model and the benchmark share design principles. Alignment with good engineering principles can legitimately produce a high score. The public evidence cannot distinguish that from tuning to benchmark-specific characteristics, because trial-level validation is impossible from the released artifacts.

**The ask.** Publish enough to make the verdicts publicly checkable: grader model and version per criterion class, pinned per revision; repeated-grader agreement on fixed submissions and per-model uncertainty intervals; per-trial submissions and rubric outcomes for a validation subset; mutagent adaptation logs for that subset; per-entry harness and effort configuration; an ownership statement; the reason for Diamond's deprecation. And the number the headline implies: criterion validity against adjudicated real merge decisions on post-cutoff PRs. Git history and PR threads supply field outcomes in bulk, though raw merge status needs adjudication first; my own data contains accepted-but-closed cases. Every finding above has a falsifier in the audit. A judge-family rank-stability ablation, for instance, resolves the grader-family half of finding 3. I'd rather link your ablation than my inference.
